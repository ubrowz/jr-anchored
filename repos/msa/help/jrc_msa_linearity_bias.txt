
What this script does:
       Answers two questions about your gauge:

         Bias:      "Does my gauge lie, and by how much?"
         Linearity: "Does my gauge lie equally at all points in its range?"

       Bias: you take a part whose true value you know exactly (from a
       reference standard or calibrated master gauge), measure it many times,
       and compare the average to the true value. The difference is the bias.
       A zero-bias gauge reads the true value on average. A biased gauge
       consistently reads high or low.

       Linearity: a gauge might be accurate at 5 mm but systematically read
       high at 10 mm and low at 2 mm. That is a linearity problem — the error
       changes depending on where in the range you are measuring. Linearity is
       detected by repeating the bias experiment at several reference points
       spread across the operating range, then fitting a straight line through
       those bias values. A flat line (slope near zero) means the gauge is
       equally accurate everywhere. A steep slope means the gauge gets
       progressively worse across the range.

       The practical question: "Can I trust this gauge across its whole
       operating range, or only in the middle?"

Description: Gauge Linearity and Bias analysis. Fits a linear regression of
             bias (measured minus reference) vs reference value across multiple
             reference points. Reports slope, per-part bias with significance
             tests, %Linearity, and %Bias. Saves a two-panel PNG to ~/Downloads/.

Usage: jrc_msa_linearity_bias <data.csv> [--tolerance <value>]

Arguments:
       data.csv             CSV file with columns: part, reference, value.
                              part      — part identifier
                              reference — known true (reference) value
                              value     — measured value
                            At least 2 parts and 2 replicates per part required.
       --tolerance <value>  Optional. Process tolerance (USL - LSL).
                            When supplied, %Bias and %Linearity are expressed
                            relative to tolerance. Otherwise expressed relative
                            to the reference value range.
       -h | --help          Show this help message

Input CSV format:
       part,reference,value
       1,10.00,10.012
       1,10.00,10.008
       2,12.50,12.531
       ...

Output — Terminal:
       Per-part bias table (mean, bias, 95% CI, p-value)
       Linearity regression (slope, intercept, R², p-values)
       Summary (%Linearity, %Bias)
       Pass/fail verdict

Output — PNG (~/Downloads/):
       Left panel:  Bias vs Reference — scatter, regression line, 95% CI band
       Right panel: Per-part bias bar chart with 95% CI error bars
                    (bars shaded orange = bias statistically significant)

Acceptance criteria (industry standard):
       %Linearity < 10%   Acceptable
       %Linearity 10-30%  Marginal
       %Linearity > 30%   Unacceptable
       %Bias < 10%        Acceptable
       %Bias 10-30%       Marginal
       %Bias > 30%        Unacceptable

       Slope p-value < 0.05 indicates statistically significant linearity error.
       Per-part p-value < 0.05 indicates significant bias at that reference point.

Examples:
       jrc_msa_linearity_bias data.csv
       jrc_msa_linearity_bias data.csv --tolerance 1.0
